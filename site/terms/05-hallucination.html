Hallucination

AI does not know what it does not know. When an LLM lacks a good answer, it does not say "I am not sure." It generates something that sounds right based on language patterns, even if it is completely false. An AI model will cite papers that do not exist and state fabricated statistics with complete confidence.

This is not a bug that will be fixed in a future update. During training, models get scored with a simple system: one point for a right answer, zero for "I don't know." Guessing always beats abstaining. The model learns that producing some answer is always the better move.

Even models trained on perfectly clean data still hallucinate. If a fact appears only once in the training data, the model cannot distinguish it from noise. If 20 percent of a given fact type appears only once, expect at least a 20 percent hallucination rate on that type.

Models are more reliable on well-known topics and more dangerous on niche ones. The thinner the training data, the more the model is guessing.

There is a proposed fix: change scoring to penalize wrong answers more than it rewards right ones. Same logic as medical licensing exams that penalize guessing. That research is still early, but it shows the problem is understood structurally.
